What is AI

Model Training

Trained on billions of text examples (books, articles, websites). AI models learn patterns in grammar, facts, reasoning, and style through extensive processing.

Companies gather billions of documents—books, Wikipedia articles, websites, research papers—and feed them through thousands of powerful computers for months. Training just one model like ChatGPT can cost over $100 million. The computer isn't memorizing facts, but learning patterns—understanding how human language works and the patterns of human thought.

Fine Tuning

After initial training, models undergo fine-tuning to teach them how to have helpful conversations. This process involves training on examples of good AI responses to make the model more useful and aligned with human expectations.

Alignment

Once trained and fine-tuned, models are tested and reviewed for quality and safety. This crucial step ensures they don't provide harmful, illegal, or inappropriate information. Alignment makes AI systems beneficial, harmless, and honest in their interactions with users.

Response Generation

After extensive testing and refinement, the model is released to the public. When you send a request, it generates responses one word at a time. For each word, it thinks: "Based on all the patterns I've learned and the current conversation, what word comes next?"

Think of it like super-powered autocomplete. It's choosing the most likely next word based on the patterns learned during training. And it does that for each subsequent word until it feels the answer is complete.

When to Use AI and When Not to Use AI

When to Use AI

There are several types of work where AI can be particularly useful, given the current capabilities and limitations of LLMs:

  1. Work that requires quantity. For example, the number of ideas you generate determines the quality of the best idea. You want to generate a lot of ideas in any brainstorming session. Most people stop after generating just a few ideas because they become exhausted but, the AI can provide hundreds that do not meaningfully repeat.
  2. Work where you are an expert and can assess quickly whether AI is good or bad. This can involve complicated and exacting work, but it relies on your expertise to determine whether the AI is providing valuable outputs. For example, o1, the new AI model from OpenAI, can solve some PhD-level problems, but it can be hard to know whether its answers are useful without being an expert yourself.
  3. Work that involves summarizing large amounts of information, but where the downside of errors is low, and you are not expected to have detailed knowledge of the underlying information. AI is good at summarizing novel-length work, but less successful at fact-checking it.
  4. Work that is mere translation between frames or perspectives. For example, you have developed a policy but now have to turn it into a dozen different training documents for different audiences in your organization. AI is very good at this sort of translation, increasing and decreasing complexity of documents so that people can understand them.
  5. Work that will keep you moving forward. Little things often block our way, and a push might be all we need to accomplish it. When writing prior to AI, I might get stuck on a sentence and walk away from writing for an hour, but now I ask AI give me thirty distinct ways to end this sentence. I would likely not use any of them verbatim, but they may unlock some ways forward I may not have thought of.
  1. Work that contains some elements that you can understand but need help on the context or details. Tyler Cowen suggests using the AI as a companion when reading, because it allows you to ask infinite questions.
  2. Work where you need variance, and where you will select the best answer as an editor or curator. Asking for a variety of solutions - give me 15 ways to rewrite this bullet in radically different styles, be creative - allows you to find ideas that might be interesting.
  3. Work that research shows that AI is almost certainly helpful in - many kinds of coding, for example.
  4. Work where you need a first pass view at what a hostile, friendly, or naive recipient might think.
  5. Work that is entrepreneurial, where you are expected to stretch your expertise widely over many different disciplines, and where the alternative to a good-enough partner is to not be able to act at all. AI can be a surprisingly competent co-founder, helping give mentorship while also acting to build the documents, demos, and approaches that are otherwise likely to be outside your experience.
  6. Work where you need a specific perspective, and where a simulated first pass from that perspective can be helpful, like reactions from fictional personas.
  7. Work that is mere ritual, long severed from its purpose (like certain standardized reports that no one reads). What, in the words of Bob Sutton and Huggy Rao, scatters your attention and makes you less valuable? What work serves no useful purpose? In an ideal world, you would remove the work, but you can at least reduce its hold on you by having AI help.
  8. Work where you want a second opinion. Give an AI access to the data and see if it reaches the same conclusion.
  9. Format conversion. From raw notes to a table, list to CSV, etc. Simple yet powerful and saves time.
  10. Work that AIs can do better than humans. This is likely to be the fastest-growing category.

Don't Use AI

We can set aside the obvious scenarios - using AI for illegal purposes, in high-stakes situations where errors could be catastrophic, or for decisions that ethically require human work. Beyond these clear-cut cases here are 5 times not to use AI:

  1. When you need to learn and synthesize new ideas or information. Asking for a summary is not the same as reading for yourself. Asking AI to solve a problem for you is not an effective way to learn, even if it feels like it should be. To learn something new, you are going to have to do the reading and thinking yourself, though you may still find an AI helpful for parts of the learning process.
  2. When very high accuracy is required. The problem with AI errors, the infamous hallucinations, is that, because of how LLMs work, the errors are going to be very plausible. Hallucinations are therefore very hard to spot, and research suggests that people don't even try, "falling asleep at the wheel" and not paying attention. Hallucinations can be reduced, but not eliminated.
  3. When you do not understand the failure modes of AI. AI doesn't fail exactly like a human. You know it can hallucinate, but that is only one form of error: AIs often try to persuade you that they are right, or they might become sycophantic and agree with your incorrect answer. You need to use AI enough to understand these risks.
  4. When the effort is the point. In many areas, people need to struggle with a topic to succeed - writers rewrite the same page, academics revisit a theory many times. By shortcutting that struggle, no matter how frustrating, you may lose the ability to reach the vital "aha" moment. A certain amount of friction seems necessary to make facts stick in the mind, and keep them from slipping down the memory hole.
  5. When AI is bad at the task. This may seem obvious, but AI is bad at things you wouldn't expect (counting the number of r's in the word "strawberry") and good at things you wouldn't expect (writing a Shakespearean sonnet about how hard it is to count the number of r's in the word strawberry where the first letter of every line spells out two fruits). Unfortunately, there is no general manual to tell you the shape of the Jagged Frontier of AI abilities, which are constantly evolving. Trial and error, and sharing information with peers, is vital to figuring this out.

Mindset Needed / It's not Google

The observation about people treating AI like Google resonates deeply. The real challenge isn't just teaching prompt engineering but reframing how people think about AI entirely.

What if AI education focused less on technical skills and more on developing an experimental mindset—treating AI as a partner to learn from through trial and error? That could bridge the gap between what these models can do and how they're actually used.

Types of Prompting

There are two main approaches to working with AI models: structured prompting and conversational prompting. Each has its strengths and ideal use cases.

Reference: Working with AI: Two Paths to Prompting

Structured Prompting

Structured prompting involves crafting comprehensive, detailed prompts that provide all necessary context, instructions, and format requirements upfront. This approach is ideal for tasks that require specific, context-rich responses.

Big Prompts

These are ideal for tasks that require structured, context-rich responses. For example, if you're asking AI to draft a business proposal, a comprehensive prompt that outlines your objectives, target audience, and key points will produce the best results.

Example: Business Proposal Request
You are an experienced business consultant specializing in SaaS marketing strategies.

TASK: Draft a comprehensive business proposal for a new AI-powered social media management tool targeting small businesses.

CONTEXT:
- Target audience: Small businesses with 5-20 employees
- Budget constraints: $300-500/month for marketing tools
- Main pain points: Time management, consistent posting, content creation
- Unique selling proposition: AI-generated content customized to brand voice
- Competitors: Hootsuite, Buffer, Later

FORMAT:
1. Executive Summary (max 150 words)
2. Problem Statement
3. Proposed Solution with 3 key features
4. Implementation Timeline (3-month plan)
5. Pricing Structure (3 tiers)
6. Expected ROI

Use professional language but avoid jargon. Include specific metrics and KPIs where appropriate.

I need to write a business proposal for a new social media tool.

I'd be happy to help with that. Could you tell me a bit more about the tool and who it's for?

It's for small businesses, and it uses AI to help with social media management.

Thanks for that information. To create a more effective proposal, I'll need some additional details:

  1. What specific pain points does this tool address?
  2. Who are your main competitors?
  3. What's your unique selling proposition?
  4. What's the price range you're considering?

Small businesses struggle with time management and consistent posting. Competitors include Hootsuite and Buffer. Our tool uses AI to generate content in the brand's voice. We're thinking $300-500/month.

Great, that's helpful. Would you like me to structure this as a formal proposal with sections like an executive summary, problem statement, solution, timeline, pricing, and ROI?

Key Benefits of Structured Prompting:
  • Precision: Greater control over the output format and content
  • Efficiency: Faster results with fewer back-and-forth exchanges
  • Consistency: More reliable results when reusing prompts
  • Complexity: Better for handling multi-part or complex tasks

With structured prompting, you're essentially researching and working hard to get a one-shot response that meets all your requirements.

Conversational Prompting

Conversational prompting involves shorter, iterative exchanges with the AI. This approach is better suited for exploratory tasks like brainstorming or generating ideas, allowing you to refine the AI's output step by step.

Casual Chats

Shorter, iterative exchanges are better suited for exploratory tasks like brainstorming or generating ideas. This approach allows you to refine the AI's output step by step, adapting your input based on its responses.

Example: Marketing Slogan Brainstorming
You are a creative marketing expert with 15+ years of experience in brand slogan development.

TASK: Generate 10 catchy, memorable marketing slogans for an eco-friendly reusable water bottle brand named "AquaEternal".

CONTEXT:
- Target audience: Environmentally conscious millennials and Gen Z
- Brand values: Sustainability, durability, design, social responsibility
- Unique features: Made from recycled ocean plastic, keeps drinks cold for 24 hours or hot for 12 hours, portion of profits donated to ocean cleanup
- Tone: Modern, inspiring, slightly playful but not silly
- Length: Each slogan should be under 8 words

FORMAT:
Provide 10 numbered slogans with a one-sentence explanation of the concept behind each.

Key Benefits of Conversational Prompting:
  • Exploration: Better for discovering ideas you hadn't considered
  • Flexibility: Easier to pivot based on intermediate results
  • Refinement: Ability to progressively improve outputs
  • Learning: Helps you understand what works through iteration

Pro Tip: Never accept the first answer. Refine responses with follow-up prompts to add specificity, change format, or improve quality.

Controlling the Conversation

The context of the conversation is important and directly impacts the AI's response. Knowing when to use a follow-up prompt versus rerunning the prompt versus starting a new chat can significantly improve your results.

Building on the Conversation

Continue the same chat thread when you want the AI to remember previous context, instructions, or examples. This is particularly useful when refining a specific output or working through a complex problem step by step.

When you want to maintain context and build upon previous exchanges

Forking the Conversation

Use the "Edit" button to try different approaches without losing the conversation history. This allows you to experiment with alternative instructions or inputs while preserving the valuable context you've built up.

When you want to try a different approach without starting over

Starting a New Conversation

Begin a fresh chat when the current context becomes confusing or is no longer relevant. This gives you a clean slate and can help when the AI seems stuck in a particular pattern or interpretation.

When context gets muddled or you need a completely fresh perspective

Prompting Frameworks

Prompting frameworks provide structured approaches to crafting effective prompts for AI systems. These frameworks will evolve as models evolve (for instance, assigning a role might not be as valuable as it was last year), but they offer valuable starting points for consistent, high-quality results.

RTCF Framework

The RTCF framework is one of the most popular and effective structured prompting approaches. It stands for Role, Task, Context, and Format, providing a comprehensive structure for complex prompts.

R

Role

Specify who the AI should pretend to be, ideally someone with expertise relevant to the task.

"You are an experienced data scientist specializing in market analysis"
T

Task

Clearly describe what you want the AI to do, being as specific as possible.

"Analyze this customer survey data and identify the top 3 pain points"
C

Context

Provide background information, constraints, and any other details the AI needs to know.

"This is for a B2B SaaS company with enterprise clients. Focus on technical issues rather than pricing concerns."
F

Format

Specify how you want the information presented (structure, style, length, etc.).

"Present your analysis as a professional report with an executive summary, methodology section, key findings, and recommendations."

RTCF Example Prompt

**Role:** You are an experienced UX researcher with expertise in mobile app design and user behavior analysis.

**Task:** Review the attached user feedback for our fitness tracking app and identify the top 5 usability issues that should be prioritized for our next update.

**Context:** Our app targets fitness enthusiasts aged 25-45 who work out at least 3 times per week. We recently added new features for workout planning and nutrition tracking, but our app store ratings have dropped from 4.6 to 4.1 stars. We have limited engineering resources, so we need to focus on the most impactful issues first.

**Format:** Present your findings as a prioritized list with:
1. Clear issue title
2. Severity rating (Critical, High, Medium, Low)
3. Brief description of the problem
4. Specific user quotes or evidence from the feedback
5. Potential quick-win solution
End with a summary paragraph

Other Common Frameworks

While RTCF is powerful, there are several other frameworks that can be effective for different purposes. These frameworks will evolve as models improve, but they provide valuable starting points.

Starter Framework

A simple but effective approach for general prompting.

Components:
  • Expert role: "You are expert [domain]"
  • Context: Background information
  • Input: What you're providing
  • Output: What you want
  • Process guidance: "Plan steps before executing, accuracy & completeness are vital."
  • Clarification mechanism: "Ask questions for clarity"

Key Prompt Structure

A structured approach focusing on context, instructions, format, and rules.

Components:
  • Context: Who you are and what you want
  • Instructions: Specific job description
  • Output format: How results should be presented
  • Rules: Do's and don'ts
  • Examples: Sample inputs and outputs

TAG Framework

A simplified framework focusing on the essentials.

Components:
  • T: Tell the AI your goal
  • A: Assign the AI a role
  • G: Give specific guidance on how to accomplish the task

Remember: These frameworks are starting points, not rigid rules. Adapt them to your specific needs and experiment to find what works best for different tasks.

General Prompting Tips

These practical tips will help you craft more effective prompts across all AI platforms. Small changes in how you communicate with AI can dramatically improve the quality, relevance, and usefulness of the responses you receive.

Output Formatting

Specify Output Format

Always tell the AI how you want information structured (bullet points, tables, paragraphs, step-by-step, etc.). This gives you control over readability.

Example

"Present your analysis as a table with three columns: Strength, Weakness, and Impact Score (1-10). Include a brief 2-3 sentence summary at the end."

Control Response Length

Specify the desired length of the response, whether it's "brief," "detailed," or a specific word count. This helps avoid overwhelming or insufficient information.

Example

"Explain quantum computing in approximately 150 words, suitable for a high school student."

Define Tone and Style

Specify the writing style and tone you want (formal, casual, technical, simple, etc.) to match your audience and purpose.

Example

"Write this email in a professional but warm tone, avoiding overly technical language but maintaining industry credibility."

Style Modifiers to Try:
  • For clarity: "Write this like you're explaining to a smart friend who's not in this field."
  • For technical content: "Use precise technical language appropriate for a senior engineer."
  • For engaging content: "Write this in a conversational, engaging style with occasional humor."
  • For formal documents: "Use a formal, academic tone with proper citations where relevant."

Context and Background

Provide Relevant Context

Give the AI necessary background information to understand your question or task properly. Mention audience, purpose, constraints, and relevant details.

Example

"I'm preparing a presentation for potential investors who are not technical. They're concerned about market size and competitive advantage rather than implementation details."

Include Examples

Provide examples of what you're looking for to help the AI understand your expectations and preferred style or approach.

Example

"Here are two examples of how I typically write conclusions for research papers: [Example 1] [Example 2]. Please follow a similar structure and tone for this new conclusion."

Define User Personas

When appropriate, create a brief user persona to help the AI understand who will be using the information.

Example

"The target user is a 45-year-old marketing professional with basic technical knowledge who wants to understand AI applications for customer segmentation without getting lost in technical details."

Effective Persona Elements:
  1. Role/Profession - Helps frame knowledge level in specific domains
  2. Technical Expertise - Guides complexity and terminology
  3. Goals/Needs - Focuses the response on relevant information
  4. Pain Points - Highlights what to address or avoid
  5. Time Constraints - Indicates whether to optimize for brevity or completeness

Language and Clarity

Be Specific and Clear

Use precise language and avoid ambiguity. The more specific your request, the more accurate the response will be.

Instead of

"Tell me about climate change."

Try

"Explain 3 specific ways climate change is affecting agricultural production in the Midwest United States, with data from the last 5 years."

Use Action Verbs

Begin your prompts with clear action verbs to make your intent unmistakable.

Effective Action Verbs
Analyze Summarize Compare Outline Generate Evaluate Explain Translate

Set Clear Constraints

Define boundaries and limitations to focus the AI's response on what you actually need.

Example

"Focus only on peer-reviewed research from the last 3 years. Do not include animal studies or preliminary research."

Useful Constraints to Consider:
  • Time periods: "Only include developments from 2020-2025"
  • Geographic focus: "Limit examples to European markets"
  • Source quality: "Only use information from academic or government sources"
  • Industry relevance: "Focus specifically on healthcare applications"
  • Budget/resource limitations: "Solutions must be implementable by a small team with limited budget"

Iteration and Refinement

Iterate and Refine

Don't expect perfect results from your first prompt. Use follow-up requests to refine and improve initial responses.

First Prompt

"Generate 5 creative social media post ideas for a coffee shop."

Follow-up Refinement

"I like idea #3. Can you expand on that concept with more specific visual details and a suggested caption that incorporates our brand voice, which is warm but slightly quirky?"

Break Down Complex Tasks

Instead of one massive prompt, break complex tasks into a series of smaller, focused prompts for better results.

Instead of

"Create a comprehensive marketing strategy for my new fitness app, including target audience analysis, competitor research, messaging, channels, budget allocation, and KPIs."

Try This Approach

"Let's develop a marketing strategy for my fitness app step by step. First, help me identify 3-4 potential target audience segments based on these user personas..."

Concept Explanation Generator

Explaining "Machine Learning"

For a 10-year-old:

Imagine teaching your dog to fetch. At first, your dog doesn't know what to do when you throw a ball. But after practicing many times, your dog learns that when you throw something, it should run after it and bring it back. The dog learned this by practicing over and over. Machine learning is like that, but for computers. Instead of teaching computers exactly what to do with strict rules, we show them lots of examples, and they figure out the patterns themselves - just like your dog learned to fetch!

For a high school student:

Machine learning is a way to make computers learn from data without being explicitly programmed for every situation. Just as you might recognize patterns in math problems after solving many similar ones, machine learning algorithms find patterns in data. For example, a computer can learn to recognize cats in photos after seeing thousands of cat pictures. Netflix uses machine learning to recommend shows based on what you've watched before, and email services use it to filter spam. It works by adjusting mathematical models based on examples until the computer gets good at making predictions or decisions.

Image Generation Tips

Creating effective prompts for AI image generators requires a different approach than text prompts. These specialized systems have their own quirks and capabilities that you'll need to understand to get the best results.

The Prompt Framework for Images

Just as with text prompts, having a structured approach to image generation can dramatically improve your results. Here's a framework that works well across most image generation platforms:

1. Subject

What is the main focus of your image? Be specific and detailed.

Examples:
  • "A rustic cabin"
  • "A Siberian husky"
  • "A Victorian-era woman"

2. Environment/Setting

Where is your subject located? What surrounds it?

Examples:
  • "in a misty forest"
  • "on a frozen lake"
  • "in a sunlit garden with roses"

3. Lighting & Atmosphere

How is the scene lit? What's the mood or time of day?

Examples:
  • "during sunset with golden hour lighting"
  • "with dramatic side lighting"
  • "foggy morning atmosphere"

4. Style & Medium

What artistic style or medium should the image emulate?

Examples:
  • "watercolor painting"
  • "photorealistic digital art"
  • "in the style of Studio Ghibli"

5. Technical Specifications

Add details about composition, camera settings, or render quality.

Examples:
  • "ultra-detailed, 8K resolution"
  • "shallow depth of field"
  • "shot with a wide-angle lens"

Example: Putting It All Together

Basic Prompt:

"A cabin in the woods"

Basic result will be generic
Enhanced Prompt:

"A rustic log cabin with a stone chimney in a misty pine forest, morning fog lingering between the trees, golden sunlight breaking through the canopy, cinematic lighting, photorealistic digital art, ultra-detailed, 8K, shallow depth of field"

Enhanced result will be specific and atmospheric

Pro Tip: Weights and Emphasis

Some image generators allow you to emphasize certain aspects of your prompt using syntax like (keyword) or keyword++ for increased emphasis, or [keyword] or keyword-- for decreased emphasis.

Example: "A portrait of a ((cybernetic)) [human] with glowing blue eyes" will emphasize the cybernetic aspects while de-emphasizing human characteristics.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

When your image generations aren't coming out as expected, try these fixes for common problems:

Problem: Generic or Bland Results

Solution:
  • Add more specific details to your prompt
  • Include artistic style references
  • Specify lighting and atmosphere
  • Add technical specifications (8K, detailed, etc.)

Problem: Anatomical Errors

Solution:
  • Explicitly mention "correct anatomy" in your prompt
  • Use negative prompts: "deformed, extra limbs, bad anatomy"
  • Request "professional character design" or "anatomical correctness"
  • Reference particular artists known for good anatomy

Problem: Text Rendering Issues

Solution:
  • Most image generators struggle with text
  • Keep requested text very short (1-3 words)
  • Specify "clear, legible text" in your prompt
  • Consider adding text in post-processing instead

Problem: Too Much Stylization

Solution:
  • Request "photorealistic" or "photograph"
  • Reduce stylization parameters if available
  • Specify camera details (e.g., "shot on Canon 5D")
  • Include "8K resolution, highly detailed" for realism

Best Practices Overview

This section brings together key insights from across this guide into a comprehensive set of best practices. These recommendations will help you use AI tools effectively, responsibly, and creatively.

Understanding the Tool

Know Your Model's Capabilities

Different AI models have different strengths, limitations, and knowledge cutoff dates. Familiarize yourself with what your chosen model can and cannot do.

Action Steps:
  • Review the model's documentation
  • Test the model with simple tasks first
  • Stay updated on new model versions

Verify Information

AI can provide plausible-sounding but incorrect information. Always verify factual claims, especially for important decisions or sensitive topics.

Action Steps:
  • Cross-check AI responses with reliable sources
  • Ask the AI to cite sources where possible
  • Use research tools when accuracy is critical

Prompting Basics

Be Specific and Clear

Vague prompts lead to generic responses. The more specific and detailed your prompt, the better the AI can tailor its response to your needs.

Action Steps:
  • Include relevant context and background
  • Specify your goals and expectations
  • Be explicit about format, tone, and style

Use Structured Frameworks

Organizing your prompts with frameworks like RTCF (Role, Task, Context, Format) leads to more consistent and useful results.

Action Steps:
  • Choose a framework that fits your task
  • Include all relevant components
  • Maintain consistent structure across similar tasks

Iteration and Refinement

Embrace Iterative Improvement

Don't expect perfect results from your first prompt. Use follow-up questions and refinements to guide the AI toward better outputs.

Action Steps:
  • Review initial responses critically
  • Ask for revisions with specific guidance
  • Build on partial successes

Provide Constructive Feedback

AI systems learn from your feedback. Clearly communicate what works and what doesn't to improve future interactions.

Action Steps:
  • Be specific about what needs improvement
  • Explain why certain outputs don't meet your needs
  • Acknowledge when the AI gets things right

Effective Tool Usage

Leverage Multiple Input Types

Modern AI systems support text, images, documents, voice, and more. Using the right input type for your task can dramatically improve results.

Action Steps:
  • Upload relevant documents for context
  • Include images when discussing visual concepts
  • Use voice mode for natural conversations

Match the Tool to the Task

Choose the right tool for each specific job. General-purpose AI is versatile, but specialized tools often perform better for specific tasks.

Action Steps:
  • Use specialized image generators for visual content
  • Try code-specific models for programming tasks
  • Consider domain-specific tools for specialized work

Caution & Judgment

Maintain Human Oversight

AI should assist your thinking, not replace it. Always apply critical judgment to AI outputs, especially for important decisions.

Action Steps:
  • Review AI suggestions before implementing them
  • Consider AI outputs as advice, not commands
  • Maintain final decision-making authority

Use AI Ethically

Consider the ethical implications of your AI use, including privacy, copyright, bias, and potential impacts on others.

Action Steps:
  • Respect intellectual property and give credit
  • Be transparent about AI use when appropriate
  • Consider the broader impacts of AI-generated content
